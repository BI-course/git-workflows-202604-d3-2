Business data sources are generally reviewed across three major dimensions: 
1.Origin (Internal vs. External)
2.Method (Primary vs. Secondary)
3.Format (Structured vs. Unstructured)

1. Internal Data Sources
These sources are generated within your company's own systems and offer the most granular, real-time look at operations. 

Transactional Systems: Core data from Enterprise Resource Planning (ERP) and Customer Relationship Management (CRM) platforms. This includes sales records, invoices, and payment logs.
Departmental Records:
Finance: Cash flow reports, budgets, and production costs.
Human Resources: Employee demographics, payroll, retention rates, and recruitment costs.
Marketing: Website traffic (e.g., Google Analytics), email open rates, and conversion metrics.
Operational Logs: Machine-generated data from IoT sensors, device connectivity, and fleet tracking.
Archives: Historical data stored in databases or physical files that provide context for trend analysis. 

2. External Data Sources
External data helps you understand the broader market, your competitors, and public sentiment. 

Government & Public Data: Open datasets from sites like Data.gov, the Census Bureau, or the CIA World Factbook.
Social & Digital Media: Unstructured data from platforms like Facebook or LinkedIn, used for sentiment analysis and tracking brand perception.
Third-Party Aggregators: Data purchased from specialized vendors (e.g., Nielsen, Bloomberg) for industry benchmarks and consumer behavior insights.
Trade Associations: Reports from Chambers of Commerce or specialized industry journals. 

3. Collection Methods
Primary Data: Original information you collect yourself for a specific purpose via surveys, interviews, focus groups, or direct observation. It is highly accurate but expensive and time-consuming.
Secondary Data: Information already collected and published by others. This includes books, news articles, and government reports. It is cost-effective and easy to access but may be outdated. 

4. Data Formats
Structured: Highly organized and easily searchable in relational databases (e.g., SQL tables, Excel spreadsheets).
Unstructured: Information without a predefined model, such as emails, PDFs, videos, and social media posts.
Semi-Structured: Data with some organizational markers like JSON or XML files.
